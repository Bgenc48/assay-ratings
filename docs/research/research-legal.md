# Legal, Regulatory & Liability Analysis — Token Trust-Rating Service
*Research date: 2026-07-10. Founder: Türkiye tax resident, solo, non-technical. Product: public algorithmic letter-grade ratings of tokens + paid monitoring/alerts. Not legal advice; flagged items need a paid opinion.*

---

## 0. Bottom lines (read this first)

1. **Publishing impersonal token ratings is legal in all target jurisdictions without a license** — provided the product stays *impersonal* (same score shown to everyone), *unpaid-by-issuers*, and free of *personalized* "buy/sell for you" output. Every regulatory trap below is triggered by drifting from one of those three properties.
2. **Credit-rating-agency regimes (SEC NRSRO, EU CRA Reg 1060/2009) do not apply** — confirmed: both are scoped to *creditworthiness of entities/debt obligations*. A token trust grade is not a credit rating. One branding rule follows: **never call it a "credit rating."**
3. **Defamation is the #1 real-world risk, and your architecture is the defense.** "Every score traceable to a verifiable on-chain fact, with the evidence displayed" is not just methodology — it is the *disclosed-facts opinion doctrine* implemented in software. Treat evidence snapshots, right-of-response, and a correction log as launch-blocking legal requirements.
4. **Form a Wyoming LLC before publishing the first F grade** (~$160–$300 year one). It is simultaneously the liability shield, the Stripe workaround for Türkiye, and the entity that keeps the rating brand outside the Turkish CMB's direct reach. Total recurring cost ≈ **$450–$700/yr** including US tax filings.
5. **Biggest hidden landmine found: Etherscan's API terms prohibit commercial use and AI/dataset use.** Build the claims engine on public RPCs, Blockscout, and commercially-licensed APIs instead (details §6).

## 1. What is a token rating, legally?

### United States
- **Investment Advisers Act:** The Act covers advice "as to securities." Two independent shields:
  - **Most tokens are not securities anymore.** The SEC/CFTC March 2026 Interpretive Release 33-11412 ([SEC press release](https://www.sec.gov/newsroom/press-releases/2026-30-sec-clarifies-application-federal-securities-laws-crypto-assets), [WilmerHale summary](https://www.wilmerhale.com/en/insights/client-alerts/20260324-the-secs-new-framework-for-crypto-assets-under-howey), [Gibson Dunn](https://www.gibsondunn.com/sec-issues-interpretive-guidance-on-application-of-federal-securities-laws-to-crypto-assets-and-related-activities/)) classifies crypto into digital commodities / collectibles / tools / stablecoins / digital securities; fungible tokens on functional networks (BTC, ETH, SOL, LINK, DOGE, etc. named explicitly) are **commodities, not securities**, absent an investment contract. Rating non-securities cannot be "investment advice as to securities."
  - **Publisher's exclusion** ([15 U.S.C. §80b-2(a)(11)(D)](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/15/80b-2); *Lowe v. SEC*, 472 U.S. 181 (1985)): bona fide publications of **general and regular circulation** giving **impersonal** analysis are excluded even for securities. Design constraints to preserve it: publish on a regular cadence, identical scores for all viewers, no "based on your portfolio" features, no issuer-paid promotional content mixed with ratings. Caution flag: the SEC's "Information Providers" request for comment signals interest in regulating highly customized analytics ([Wilson Sonsini](https://www.wsgr.com/en/insights/informationor-advice-sec-regulation-of-information-providers-may-expand-to-include-providers-of-innovative-investment-analytics.html)) — a future "score my wallet's holdings with personalized risk advice" feature would erode the exclusion. Alerts on objective events ("admin key changed") are fine; "you should sell" is not.
  - **CFTC/CTA:** spot-token commentary isn't commodity-interest advice; the impersonal-publisher exclusion exists there too. Non-issue if you never touch futures/swaps advice.
- **NRSRO:** Registration is voluntary and scoped to *credit* ratings — assessments of ability to pay financial obligations on debt instruments ([SEC](https://www.sec.gov/answers/nrsro.htm), [Investor.gov](https://www.investor.gov/introduction-investing/general-resources/news-alerts/alerts-bulletins/investor-bulletins/updated-8)). Token trust grades are outside it. **Verified: no trigger.** Branding rule: use "trust rating/grade," avoid the term "credit rating" and avoid imitating S&P/Moody's trade-dressed symbols (AAA/Aaa) too closely — use your own scale (e.g., A–F with stages, like L2Beat).

### European Union
- **MiCA:** "Providing advice on crypto-assets" — the only licensable analysis-shaped CASP service — is defined in Art. 3 as giving **personalised recommendations to a client** ([ESMA rulebook](https://www.esma.europa.eu/publications-and-data/interactive-single-rulebook/mica/article-3-definitions), [Stuchlíková analysis](https://www.stuchlikova.com/en/blog/2024/provision-of-advice-related-to-crypto-assets-according-to-mica-service-content/)). A public rating identical for everyone is not personalised → **not a CASP service, no MiCA authorization needed.** MiCA's marketing-communication rules bind *issuers and offerors*, not third-party raters ([Norton Rose MiCA guide](https://www.nortonrosefulbright.com/en/knowledge/publications/2cec201e/regulating-crypto-assets-in-europe-practical-guide-to-mica)).
- **Market Abuse Regulation "investment recommendation" rules do not apply to crypto-assets** (MAR covers financial instruments; crypto commentary sits outside it — a recognized gap). MiCA has its own market-abuse title but it targets manipulation/insider dealing, which matters for §5 (don't trade around your own rating changes), not for publishing itself.
- **CRA Regulation 1060/2009:** "credit rating" = opinion on the creditworthiness of an entity/debt obligation/debt security using an established ranking system ([EUR-Lex summary](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/EN/legal-content/summary/credit-rating-agencies.html)). Tokens aren't debt → **outside scope. Verified.**
- **⚠ Watch item — the ESG Ratings Regulation (EU) 2024/3005, applicable since 2 July 2026** ([EUR-Lex](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2024/3005/oj/eng), [ESMA scope tool](https://www.esma.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2026-02/esg_scope_decision_tool.html)): it regulates any "opinion or score… regarding governance factors… based on an established methodology and defined ranking system." A "trust rating" that scores *team/governance* behavior arguably brushes this definition. Mitigations: (a) frame dimensions as **protocol/technical integrity** ("admin-key risk," "supply integrity," "claims-vs-chain gap"), not corporate ESG/governance; (b) the regulation reaches non-EU providers mainly when they distribute **by subscription/contract to EU-regulated financial undertakings** — while you're selling to retail/devs, exposure is minimal. Revisit before signing any EU bank/fund as an API customer. This regulation is also the best evidence that the EU *will* eventually regulate crypto ratings the same way — your "open methodology + no issuer-pays" posture is exactly what it mandates, so you'd be pre-compliant.

### United Kingdom
- **Regulated advice** (RAO Art. 53) requires personal recommendation on a specified investment; most tokens aren't specified investments and generic ratings aren't personal recommendations → not caught.
- **The real UK trap is the cryptoasset financial-promotion regime** (since Oct 2023, criminal offence, up to 2 years): any "invitation or inducement to engage in investment activity" in qualifying cryptoassets aimed at UK consumers must be approved/communicated by an authorised or registered firm ([FCA PS23/6](https://www.fca.org.uk/publications/policy-statements/ps23-6-financial-promotion-rules-cryptoassets), [Dentons overview](https://www.dentons.com/en/insights/newsletters/2024/april/25/the-financial-services-and-markets-act-2023/the-uks-cryptoassets-financial-promotions-regime-everything-you-need-to-know)). Neutral, non-promotional ratings and journalism are not inducements. **What would convert your site into an illegal promotion: exchange affiliate/referral links, "buy here" buttons, sponsored token placements, or promoting a specific token's merits.** Design rules: no affiliate links to trading venues, no "top tokens to buy" framing, ratings pages must read as assessment, not solicitation. (This also protects the brand's neutrality, so the incentive is aligned.)

### Türkiye (founder's residence — cannot be ignored)
- Law No. 7518 (July 2024) put crypto under the Capital Markets Board (CMB/SPK); licensing applies to **issuance, trading, custody, transfer** — an information/ratings publisher is not a CASP ([CMS](https://cms.law/en/tur/legal-updates/tuerkiye-introduces-new-crypto-asset-regulations-a-landmark-development-in-digital-finance), [Legal500 Türkiye guide](https://www.legal500.com/guides/legal-landscapes/turkey-blockchain-crypto-assets/)).
- But Türkiye criminalizes **unauthorized investment advisory** (3–5 years imprisonment) and the SPK has pursued social-media finfluencers; the line drawn in practice: **directive recommendations to specific persons/paid groups** = advisory; general impersonal commentary = not ([practitioner analysis](https://bilalalyar.av.tr/en/influencerlarin-yatirim-tavsiyesi-verme-sucu-ve-dikkat-etmeleri-gerekenler)). Paid Telegram-style "premium signal groups" are exactly what gets prosecuted — **your paid tier must sell monitoring/alerts on objective events, never buy/sell calls.**
- Practical posture: operate the product through the foreign entity, English-language, no Türkiye-targeted marketing, standard "yatırım tavsiyesi değildir" (not investment advice) disclaimer, and keep the paid product event-based. Residual risk: low, but get 1–2 hours of Turkish capital-markets counsel review before launch (~$300–600).

## 2. Defamation / business disparagement — the real risk, and the architecture that defeats it

**The doctrine (US):** Opinions are protected unless they imply false undisclosed facts (*Milkovich v. Lorain Journal*). The controlling rule for you: **an opinion based on fully disclosed, true facts cannot be defamatory, no matter how harsh** ([Minc Law](https://www.minclaw.com/opinion-defense/), [FindLaw](https://constitution.findlaw.com/amendment1/defamation-and-false-statements-under-the-first-amendment.html)). What *is* actionable: the underlying factual assertions themselves ("liquidity is not locked," "the team dumped") if false. Disclaimers ("in our opinion") do **not** immunize verifiable factual claims — courts pierced exactly that in short-seller cases ([Quinn Emanuel](https://www.quinnemanuel.com/the-firm/publications/that-is-not-an-opinion-how-to-sue-short-sellers/)).

**Instructive precedents:**
- *Abu Dhabi Commercial Bank v. Morgan Stanley* (S.D.N.Y.): rating agencies **lost** broad First Amendment protection because ratings went to a select investor group, not the public; S&P paid $77M to settle ([RGRD](https://www.rgrdlaw.com/cases-cheyne-landmark-settlement-morgan-stanley.html)). **Corollary in your favor: free, public ratings get the strongest protection.** Keep the letter grade and its factual basis public even if analytics are paid.
- Short-seller litigation: courts let claims proceed where reports asserted specific verifiable facts that were false, and plaintiffs used the defendant's **profit motive** to argue actual malice ([ABA Business Law Today](https://businesslawtoday.org/2025/07/judicial-scrutiny-intensifies-evolving-role-short-seller-reports-securities-claims/)). Corollary: **never hold or trade positions around rating actions** (§5) — motive is the plaintiff's best weapon.

**Design requirements (treat as launch-blocking specs, not legal nice-to-haves):**
1. **Fact/opinion separation in the data model.** Every scored dimension = (verifiable fact, evidence link, timestamp, method) + (grade = labeled opinion derived by published formula). The UI shows the chain: claim → on-chain check → result → grade.
2. **Evidence snapshots.** Archive the tx hash / contract state / docs page (with capture date) behind every negative finding. In litigation, truth is an absolute defense — this is your truth file.
3. **LLM output verification gate.** The claims engine is your biggest defamation surface: a hallucinated "team wallet dumped 40%" is a false factual assertion published at scale. Requirement: **no extracted claim or verification verdict is published without a deterministic on-chain check or a human review**; low-confidence extractions get "unverified claim" status, never a negative assertion.
4. **Calibrated language.** "We could not verify X" / "no lock found at the stated address as of block N" instead of "X lied." Findings phrased as absence-of-evidence where that's what the check shows.
5. **Right of response + correction process (§3):** pre-publication notification is optional (short-sellers don't), but a *post-publication* documented dispute channel with a fixed SLA both deflects litigation and is a credibility feature. Log every correction publicly (methodology-style changelog).
6. **Forum choice:** US LLC + ToS choosing a strong anti-SLAPP state's law and courts (California, Texas, Nevada) gives early-dismissal + fee-shifting leverage against nuisance suits ([FIRE overview](https://www.fire.org/research-learn/defamation-and-first-amendment)). Note: token projects will often qualify as public figures on matters of public concern → they must prove actual malice.
7. **Insurance (defer, then buy):** media-liability/E&O for publishers runs roughly $2–6k/yr (crypto-adjacent may be higher); buy it when revenue exists or the first legal threat arrives, whichever is first.
8. **Non-US exposure:** England's defamation law is plaintiff-friendlier (though "honest opinion" and "publication on a matter of public interest" defenses exist, and serious-harm threshold helps); Türkiye has criminal insult provisions. The US entity + US forum clause doesn't stop foreign suits but raises the plaintiff's cost dramatically. Accept as residual risk.

## 3. Terms of Service & site-legal essentials (concrete checklist)

- **No-advice / no-reliance:** ratings are opinions on verifiable properties, not investment advice or an offer/solicitation; not a fiduciary; users bear own decisions ("DYOR" formalized). Add the TR-form disclaimer and a note that grades are *not* predictions of price or of non-rug behavior.
- **"Opinions based on disclosed facts" clause:** state explicitly that grades are opinions derived by the published methodology from the cited evidence — this contractualizes the defamation defense.
- **Accuracy/AS-IS:** data from public blockchains and third-party sources; may be delayed, incomplete, wrong; methodology versioned; grades change without notice. Warranty disclaimer + liability cap (fees paid or $100) + consequential-damage exclusion.
- **Dispute & correction process (your "DMCA-style" ask):** a project-facing portal: (1) claimant identifies the specific factual statement disputed, (2) must attach verifiable evidence (tx hash, contract address, signed message from the project's known address — nice touch: authenticate disputants by wallet signature), (3) 10-business-day review SLA, (4) outcomes: correct + changelog entry, annotate ("disputed by issuer, our verification stands"), or no action with reasons. Publish the policy. **Right-of-response:** every rated project may post one linked official response on its rating page.
- **Actual DMCA:** only needed if you host user-generated content; agent registration is $6 via the Copyright Office — do it if you add comments/community notes.
- **API license terms:** attribution required ("Trust data by X"); no resale/redistribution/syndication of bulk scores; no use of the API to build a competing rating dataset or train models; rate limits; scores must be displayed current-and-linked (see next item); revocable on breach. (Mirror of CoinGecko's own structure — [CoinGecko API terms](https://www.coingecko.com/en/api_terms).)
- **Badge/embed license (integrity-critical):** projects will want "Rated A" badges. License terms: badge must fetch the *live* grade, may not display a stale or selective grade, no use implying endorsement; violation = trademark claim. Register the wordmark early (US federal filing ~$350/class; do it once revenue justifies).
- **Arbitration + class waiver + governing law/forum** (Delaware or the anti-SLAPP forum) for the US entity; **Privacy policy:** you process almost no personal data — keep it that way. One GDPR/KVKK note: naming individual founders and linking wallet addresses to named EU persons is personal-data processing; rely on journalistic/legitimate-interest bases, stick to publicly self-disclosed roles.

**Recommended path:**
1. **Form a Wyoming single-member LLC now, before first publication** — the defamation shield alone justifies it; it also solves payments. Get EIN (free, Form SS-4 by fax, 2–6 weeks without SSN). Wyoming over Delaware: cheaper ($60 vs $300/yr) and owner-anonymous ([Wyoming LLC costs](https://www.businessrocket.com/business-corner/start/llc/cost-wyoming/)). No BOI/CTA filing currently required for domestic LLCs (FinCEN 2025 interim rule) — reconfirm at filing time.
2. **Banking:** Mercury first (warning: 2026 Mercury is rejecting registered-agent-only virtual addresses — be ready with a real business narrative and use a non-CMRA virtual office); fallbacks that are approving non-residents: **Airwallex, Wise Business, Relay** ([2026 banking guide](https://www.internationcorpus.com/post/us-bank-account-for-non-residents-2026)).
3. **Payments:** Stripe under the LLC. **Cheaper de-risked alternative for day one: a merchant of record (Paddle, Lemon Squeezy, Polar) — these work for Türkiye-based sellers, handle global VAT/sales tax, and can even remove the need for the US bank account initially** ([TR SaaS payments overview](https://ceaksan.com/en/saas-payment-infrastructure-turkey)). MoR fee ~5% + payment fees vs Stripe ~2.9–4.4%; at your early volume the difference is noise. Note: an information-SaaS about crypto is not a prohibited "crypto business" for these processors (no custody/exchange), but disclose the business accurately at onboarding.
4. **US tax:** foreign-owned single-member LLC with no US office/agents ⇒ no ECI ⇒ **$0 US income tax**; mandatory **Form 5472 + pro-forma 1120** annually ($25k penalty if missed — the one deadline that matters) ([Cleer Tax](https://www.cleertax.com/resources/foreign-owned-llc/), [LLCU 5472 guide](https://www.llcuniversity.com/irs/form-5472-foreign-owned-llc/)).
5. **Türkiye tax (the one paid opinion to buy, ~$500–1,500):** you're taxed on worldwide income. Key open question: whether TR characterizes the disregarded LLC as transparent (income = your personal commercial income, potentially eligible for the 80% service-export deduction) or as a foreign corporation (CFC rules, Art. 7 KVK: ≥50% control + ≥25% passive income + <10% foreign tax). An active-service business likely avoids CFC's passive-income prong, but get it in writing from a Turkish CPA and declare consistently. There is a US–Türkiye income tax treaty (1996) preventing double taxation.

## 5. Conflict of interest — ATA Coin

The credibility posture (unpurchasable scores) dies instantly if COI handling looks self-serving. Adopt the strictest version; it's cheap:

1. **ATA is not rated — or algorithmic-only with a conflict banner.** Cleanest: ATA's page shows "**Not Rated — Founder Conflict of Interest**" with a link to the COI policy, plus the raw dimension data (facts only, no grade). Second-best: publish the purely algorithmic score with a permanent banner "The founder of this service is the founder of ATA; this grade is computed with zero manual input; audit the inputs here." Do NOT quietly rate it well.
2. **Site-wide disclosure**, not per-page fine print: methodology page lists founder's token holdings/affiliations (ATA), updated on change. This mirrors the IOSCO CRA Code and EU CRA Reg disclosure norms and the SPK's general-recommendation principles.
3. **Rating ATA's competitors:** legally permissible (opinion on disclosed facts), but this is where a disparagement plaintiff finds **motive** — precisely the actual-malice fuel used against short sellers. Mitigations: identical published methodology applied mechanically; the competitor's page carries an automatic disclosure ("operator holds a founder position in a token in this category"); any human-judgment override on a competitor's grade is prohibited (algorithmic output only).
4. **No-trading rule:** founder/staff do not trade any rated token within N days around that token's rating actions, and never short rated tokens. Publish it. This also keeps you clear of MiCA/CMB market-abuse theories.
5. **Seed methodology reuse is fine** — "our methodology grew out of the adversarial trust-gap review built for ATA" is a credibility asset if disclosed, a scandal if discovered.

## 6. Data & content legalities for the claims engine

**Scraping project docs/websites:**
- CFAA risk for public pages is low post-*hiQ* (scraping public data isn't "unauthorized access"), but *hiQ* ultimately **lost on breach of contract** — a $500k judgment for violating ToS it had accepted ([ZwillGen wrap-up](https://www.zwillgen.com/alternative-data/hiq-v-linkedin-wrapped-up-web-scraping-lessons-learned/), [Farella](https://www.fbm.com/publications/what-recent-rulings-in-hiq-v-linkedin-and-other-cases-say-about-the-legality-of-data-scraping/)). Operating rules: **never create accounts or click-accept ToS to reach docs; scrape only public pages; respect robots.txt; low request rates; stop on cease-and-desist for that site** (you can still rate the project on chain data + note "project blocks verification" — which is itself signal).
- **Copyright:** the *facts and claims* in a whitepaper are not copyrightable; quoting short claim excerpts for verification/criticism is core fair use (transformative, non-substitutive, commentary). Don't republish whole documents; keep full-page snapshots as *private* litigation evidence and cite archive.org publicly. Post-*Thomson Reuters v. Ross* (fair use denied where copying built a substitute product), your position is distinguishable: you're verifying issuers' public marketing claims, not repackaging their expressive content.
- Practical note: projects *want* their docs read — expect near-zero enforcement appetite, except from projects you grade F, which is why the rules above matter.

**Chain & market data:**
- **⚠ Etherscan API: terms restrict content to personal, non-commercial use and expressly prohibit AI/ML/dataset use without written permission** ([Etherscan API terms](https://etherscan.io/apiterms)). Do not build the product on it. Alternatives: **public JSON-RPC endpoints (permissionless — reading chain state has no ToS), Blockscout (open source), Alchemy/Infura/QuickNode free tiers (commercial use allowed under their terms), The Graph.** Your existing ATA chain-reading TypeScript on public RPCs is already the compliant pattern. Base (your home chain) has good public RPC + Blockscout coverage.
- **CoinGecko API:** commercial use allowed on paid plans with **"Data provided by CoinGecko" attribution + link**; no redistribution/resale of their data — so your API product must expose *your scores*, not pass through their market data ([CoinGecko commercial license](https://support.coingecko.com/hc/en-us/articles/16760512207257-What-are-the-differences-between-commercial-and-custom-licenses)). Same review needed for any CEX API (Binance/Coinbase terms similarly bar redistribution).
- **DEX data:** read pools/locks directly on-chain via RPC — no license needed, and it's the more defensible evidence anyway.
